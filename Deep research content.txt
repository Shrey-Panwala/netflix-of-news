AI-Native News Experience (“Netflix of News”)
The goal is to transform static news into an AI-native, personalized “Netflix-like” platform. Our solution goes far beyond a simple news app or chatbot. We will build a multi-agent system that fetches, understands, and re-presents news in engaging, customized formats (text, video, Q&A) for each user. This means deep personalization, real-time reasoning, and rich multimedia output – making news consumption efficient, interactive and globally accessible. In hackathon terms, we must deliver a clear prototype with source code, architecture diagram, documentation and a demo video
.

We address the problem that today’s news is “static, same for everyone, hard to consume.” Our “AI News OS” will curate a continuous news feed that adapts to each person’s interests (stocks, industries, region) and explains why each story matters to them. Think of it as a personal financial-news concierge: “Here are 5 things that will move your portfolio today,” or a briefing voice that narrates market highlights for your watchlist.
The platform integrates multiple channels: while ET is a key content partner, our design is channel-agnostic – any news provider (global outlets, local papers, specialized feeds) can plug in. In effect, we create a universal news layer powered by AI that benefits all partners and users worldwide.
Global AI News Landscape
Several news organizations and startups are already experimenting with AI for news. For example, Aftonbladet (Sweden) uses AI to automatically summarize articles, and the Miami Herald converts text news to audio
. Argentina’s Clarín newspaper employs an AI tool (UalterAI) that not only summarizes content but also extracts key quotes, figures, and glossaries for complex articles
. Major outlets like the New York Times have introduced internal tools: NYT’s “Echo” assists journalists by summarizing articles and generating social headlines
, though NYT strictly prohibits any AI-generated articles without human review
.

User surveys show strong demand for such features: in a Reuters Institute study, the most desired AI-driven news features were concise summaries (27%) and translation services (24%)
. Notably, over one-third of Indian users wanted AI to simplify language or reading level for news content
 – reflecting India’s diverse linguistic audience. These findings validate core features we should prioritize (summaries, translation, personalization) while remaining mindful of journalistic standards and human oversight
.

Key Examples and Insights
AI News Agents: Open-source projects like TyrelM10/ai-news-agent show a multi-agent design, using LangGraph to orchestrate news retrieval and LLM summarization
. Such pipelines fetch articles via News APIs and produce concise human-readable summaries, demonstrating how modular agents can work in tandem.
Automated Video Reports: Reuters worked with Synthesia to create a virtual AI news anchor for sports coverage. In their prototype, a synthetic avatar of a sports editor reads match summaries (pulled from real game data and press photos) without any human shooting or editing
. This pioneered “fully-automated” video news. It shows that text-plus-data inputs can be turned into narrated, captioned video segments – a capability we will incorporate for short news clips and explainers
.
Video Generation Tools: By late 2025, AI video generators (Synthesia, HeyGen, Canva AI, etc.) can convert scripts or even raw articles into 60-second social videos with avatars, voiceover, and charts
. These tools let us rapidly produce content like “Stocks to Watch Today” or “Budget 2026 Explained,” with animated charts and narrators (human-like or even user-customized faces).
User Interest Variations: Research shows younger audiences (under 35) are most receptive to AI news tools
. Also, offering choice in personalization matters – algorithms should inject some “randomness” to avoid echo chambers
. For instance, we might occasionally surface a surprising but relevant story outside a user’s routine preferences, keeping the “feed” rich and not just a filter bubble.
Core Features
To stand out, the platform will combine multiple innovative capabilities:

Dynamic Summaries & Briefings: Rather than listing headlines, the AI will present custom briefings. E.g., “Good morning! Market movers: Oil price up 2%, tech stocks rally – here’s what it means for your portfolio.” Users can click for drill-down details or ask follow-up questions (“Why did oil jump?”) via a chat/voice interface. This Q&A capability means users can interact naturally with the news (“the News Navigator”).

Hyper-Personalization Engine: Each user’s profile, portfolio, and reading history feeds an AI model that ranks and filters stories. Using embedded vectors and RAG techniques, we tailor content by industry, region, sentiment, or even tone preference. For example, the system might flag “3 companies you follow are in the news” and highlight those stories. We can cite industry interest: personalizing news homepages is common, but generative AI lets us go further (e.g. converting content into preferred formats)
.

Multilingual Accessibility: The AI will translate or rephrase news into multiple languages and reading levels. For India, support for Hindi, regional languages, or simple-English variants is critical. Tools like Claude 3.5 Sonnet (trained for multilingual output) can be used as in Lok͞al’s case study: Lok͞al built a Telugu-news summarizer with Claude Sonnet, achieving 78% reduction in editorial effort and expanding to other languages
. We’ll similarly tune models for key languages and validate outputs with prompts (checking grammar/cultural fit before publishing
).

AI Video & Multimedia Output: Each summarized story can be repurposed into AI-generated video or audio. For example, the app could auto-create a 60-second video briefing (“market news reel”), using animated charts and voice-over, or generate a podcast-style audio summary. Libraries like Synthesia or HeyGen provide APIs for on-the-fly video generation
. We’ll integrate such tools so users can “play” a news summary video instead of reading text. For social sharing, an “AI Reel” creator can take any news item and make a short explainer video.

Interactive Story Explorer: A unique feature (“Story Arc Tracker”) will let users dive into ongoing stories like following episodes of a series. For instance, a timeline view of “The Adani case” or “AI race – OpenAI vs Google” can show key events, AI-generated sentiment/trend graphs, and even AI-generated predictions of next developments. Users can ask questions at any point in the timeline for deeper context.

Voice & Chat Interface: Beyond visual feeds, we’ll offer a conversational agent (chatbot or voice assistant) that users can query. “What’s happening in renewable energy stocks?” or “Explain this morning’s cyberattack news.” The backend will use a RAG pipeline: retrieve relevant news docs, then generate answers with LLMs. Having a multi-turn conversation (with memory of prior queries) makes the experience richer than one-off summaries.

Social/Collaboration Layer: Optionally, users could bookmark or comment on AI-generated insights, or share them within the app (like “tags” on Netflix shows). To differentiate, we might allow community “news playlists” on topics.

System Architecture & Tech Stack
Our solution uses a multi-agent, RAG-based architecture
. The high-level flow is:

User Profile + Content Feeds --> Agentic Pipeline --> Personalized Output (text/video/app)
Ingestion: A set of News Ingestion Agents continuously pull content from RSS/News APIs (ET’s feeds, Bloomberg, Reuters, local outlets, etc.) and filter/clean it. This ensures up-to-date coverage.
Vector Store & RAG: Articles are embedded (e.g. with OpenAI/FAISS) into a vector database (e.g. Pinecone/ElasticSearch). This supports fast retrieval of relevant articles when answering queries or generating digests
.
Router Agent: Follows the LangChain “Routing” pattern
. It examines each user query or task (e.g. “summarize 3 articles” or “find context about Budget”), then decides which data sources and agents are needed (full docs, web search, or cached embeddings).
Summarizer/Analyst Agents: Specialized agents apply LLMs to perform tasks: summarization, sentiment analysis, translation, or Q&A. For summarization, we’ll use GPT-4 / Claude 3.5 with prompt engineering (guided by the Reuters framework of “cover 5Ws/1H”
). They output concise, factual summaries. A feedback loop checks for hallucinations (see below)
.
Personalization Agent: This agent matches content to the user profile. It ranks stories by relevance and generates personalized highlights (e.g. “3 stocks you track are in the news”).
Video Generation Agent: When triggered (e.g. user requests or daily news reel), this agent invokes an AI video API. It inputs the text summary and data (charts, images), then retrieves a ready-made voice/avatar to produce a short video
.
Interactive/Conversational Agent: Implements the chat interface. It takes user queries, leverages the summarizer and RAG pipeline to answer contextually, and may delegate follow-ups to itself (LangChain-style chaining).
System Orchestrator: Oversees the workflow (using LangChain/Agents or a framework like CrewAI). It handles scheduling, error handling, and ensures tasks run in parallel or sequentially as needed
.

Personalization & Content Curation
Our personalization approach avoids simple “more-of-the-same” feeds. Using the Rochester study insight, we’ll inject controlled randomness and diversity to break filter bubbles
. For example, besides recommending stories that match a user’s profile, we also include a few “surprise” articles from tangentially related beats. The user can also set preferences (“I want more global market stories, less local sports”) which the AI agent respects.

Profiles: Users optionally enter interests (sectors, stocks, regions) and their reading level. The system learns over time from clicks/reads. Each profile entry is vectorized so the “Router Agent” can match content.

Content Signals: We’ll integrate stock/commodities APIs so the AI can tie news to market data (as in “3 things affecting your portfolio today”). We’ll also implement real-time sentiment analysis (e.g. NLP agents extracting bullish/bearish tone) to surface “market sentiment gauges.”

Avoiding Bias/Echo Chambers: To mitigate algorithmic bias and polarization, we include controls: (a) use a mix of sources (national/global) and cross-check conflicting reports, (b) allow users to see “Why this story?” explanations (model reasons) to build trust, and (c) follow Proma et al.’s strategy of randomization
. This means periodically adding a relevant counterpoint or contrasting viewpoint to the personalized feed, keeping the experience broad but still relevant.

Multilingual & Accessibility
Our platform will support multiple languages and reading levels. We incorporate translation and simplification agents:

Translation Agent: For non-English stories, a dedicated LLM translation model converts them into the user’s language, maintaining context and nuance. We can fine-tune models on news corpora or use APIs (like Google’s).
Adaptive Language Agent: As in the Reuters study, many Indian users prefer simpler language
. We’ll allow users to set a desired reading level; an AI agent will rewrite articles accordingly (e.g. using a prompt like “rephrase this for an 8th-grade reader”).
Quality Control: Each translated/simplified output passes a check (grammar, accuracy) similar to Lok͞al’s validation step
. We might automatically compare key facts (dates, names) against the original to ensure no distortion.
Providing Indian regional languages (Hindi, Tamil, etc.) greatly expands reach. We will leverage open models (like IndicBERT, BLOOM) and any available corpora. This feature not only aids non-English speakers, but also aligns with hackathon emphasis on Indian use-cases
.

Video & Multimedia Generation
To make news engaging, we generate multimedia:

Auto News Videos: For major stories, the system can generate short explainer videos. It uses our Video Agent to combine narrated voice (AI TTS or synthetic avatar) with visuals (charts, stock tickers, relevant images). For example, feeding “Budget 2026 highlights” to Synthesia yields an animated briefing. This leverages GenAI research that such tools can replace days of production
.
Data-Driven Graphics: The platform will automatically create charts/visuals (stock movement graphs, infographics) from data feeds. These are included in both text summaries and video slides.
Audio Summaries/Podcasts: We can optionally output an audio version of daily news tailored to each profile. Text-to-speech models (like ElevenLabs, Tacotron) ensure a human-like voice. This caters to users on the go.
All generated media will include proper credits (e.g. "AI-generated with Synthesia").

Edge Cases, Failure Modes & Mitigation
Building “AI-powered news” comes with pitfalls. We plan comprehensive safeguards:

Hallucinations & Misinformation: Pure LLM outputs can fabricate facts. To prevent this, every AI-generated summary or answer will be grounded in real sources. We use RAG: before answering, the agent retrieves relevant articles and cites them. If no strong match is found, the agent will either decline to answer or flag for review. (Studies show that RAG and knowledge constraints significantly reduce hallucinations
.) For example, if the AI is summarizing a company’s earnings report, it will fetch the official press release and then summarize – rather than rely solely on the model’s memory.
Citation & Verification: Wherever possible, we attach links or source references in the UI (“Based on [TechCrunch article, March 2026]”). This transparency helps users trust the AI’s output. A “Did we get it right?” feedback button can catch errors and iteratively improve the model.
Echo Chamber / Polarization: As noted, news personalization risks reinforcing bias. We’ll mitigate this by algorithm design that occasionally diversifies content
. In practice, we’ll show users a mix of recommended and “editor’s pick” stories, and let them toggle broader news modes.
Content Quality & Bias: The system must filter out fake or inappropriate news. We will maintain source credibility scores (favor established outlets) and use fact-check APIs if available. Very sensitive topics (medical, legal) may come with disclaimers or be routed to domain-specific experts.
Performance/Scalability: Generating videos or running LLMs can be resource-intensive. We’ll use asynchronous processing and caching. For example, once a user’s daily briefing is generated, it’s cached for later viewing. We’ll also limit complex tasks to high-impact stories. The backend will autoscale (cloud GPU nodes for heavy AI tasks, autoscaling WebSocket servers for chat).
User Privacy: Personalization implies tracking interests. We must handle data ethically: profiles and reading habits are kept securely, and users can delete history. No sensitive personal info is stored; user queries go through the AI only for delivering the news experience and are not used to train our models beyond aggregated improvements.
Legal/Compliance: We must respect copyright. We’ll use content through official channels (news APIs or scraped text with fair use disclaimers) and avoid unauthorized text-to-speech from full articles. Any use of images or video assets will follow licensing (e.g. royalty-free charts). And we clearly label AI-generated content per NYT’s guidelines
.
Technical Failures: If an AI service is down (e.g. GPT API fails), we’ll have fallbacks: perhaps simpler extractive summaries or cached content. If translation fails for a rare language, we can display the original and an apology. The UI will gracefully degrade (show text instead of video) and communicate to users (“Generating your video…”).
